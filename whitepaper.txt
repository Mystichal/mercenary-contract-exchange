MERCENARY CONTRACT EXCHANGE

A Programmable Market for Operational Risk
Built for EVE Frontier
Settled on Sui
Developed for the ecosystem of CCP Games


1. Abstract

This paper presents a decentralized mercenary contract marketplace that enables tribes to issue operational missions, mercenary groups to execute them, and traders to price and exchange mission risk as financial instruments.

Unlike traditional mission systems, contracts are:
	pre-funded
	transferable
	objectively verified
	automatically settled
	denominated in native EVE tokens

The system transforms operational activity into a tradable asset class, enabling price discovery for military risk, logistics execution, and territorial control.

The result is a programmable war economy embedded directly into persistent world infrastructure.


2. Problem Statement

Large-scale coordination of military and logistical activity requires:
	capital commitment
	trust in execution
	objective performance verification
	risk pricing
	liquidity

Traditional mission systems lack:
	enforceable economic guarantees
	transferable obligations
	market-driven pricing
	automated settlement

This creates inefficiencies:
	underpriced risk
	unreliable execution
	limited capital participation
	no secondary market for operational exposure

A financial layer is required to allocate capital toward mission execution efficiently.


3. System Overview

The Mercenary Contract Exchange is a programmable infrastructure that converts missions into financial instruments.

Each contract represents a conditional obligation with locked economic value.

Lifecycle:
	Tribe defines mission
	Reward locked in escrow (EVE tokens)
	Contract issued
	Contract traded or assigned
	Mission executed
	World state verifies outcome
	Settlement distributes funds

No trust, no manual arbitration, no external enforcement.


4. Core Design Principles

4.1 Pre-Funded Execution
All rewards exist before execution begins.

4.2 Objective Verification
World events determine success or failure.

4.3 Transferable Obligations
Contracts can be bought, sold, or reassigned.

4.4 Automatic Settlement
Smart infrastructure distributes funds deterministically.

4.5 Unified Currency
All economic activity denominated in EVE tokens.


5. Economic Unit: EVE Token

All financial activity uses a single native settlement asset.

Used for:
	mission rewards
	mercenary collateral
	contract trading
	insurance premiums
	penalties
	platform fees

This creates:
	unified liquidity
	transparent pricing
	systemic economic coupling
	measurable capital flows


6. Financial Structure

6.1 Reward Pool
Locked by mission issuer.
Represents payment for successful execution.

6.2 Performance Collateral
Locked by executor.
Incentivizes completion and compensates failure.

6.3 Optional Market Liquidity
Third parties may buy or sell exposure to contract outcomes.

6.4 Insurance Layer
Risk transfer through premium-based coverage.


7. Contract Lifecycle

Stage 1 — Creation
Issuer defines:
	objective conditions
	deadline
	reward size
	location parameters

Reward transferred to escrow.

Stage 2 — Acceptance or Trade
Possible paths:
	mercenary accepts directly
	contract sold to trader
	pooled funding
	syndicated execution

Ownership transferable.

Stage 3 — Execution
Real world activity produces verifiable events.

Examples:
	asset destruction
	territorial control change
	delivery confirmation
	survival duration

Stage 4 — Verification
World state evaluated against contract conditions.

Binary result:
	SUCCESS or FAILURE

Stage 5 — Settlement
Automatic redistribution of EVE tokens.

Success:
	reward → executor
	collateral returned

Failure:
	collateral slashed
	reward returned or redistributed

10. Risk Engine

Collateral requirements determined algorithmically.

Example model:
	collateral = reward × risk_score

Risk score derived from:
	combat density index
	travel exposure
	historical loss rates
	objective complexity

Dynamic adjustment prevents under-collateralization.
